# Research Narrative

**Title:** The Gies AI Innovation Engine: Shared Research Infrastructure for AI-Driven Business Education

**PI:** Vishal Sachdev, Clinical Associate Professor, Business Administration
**Co-Is:** Qingquan Zhang, Adjunct Clinical Assistant Professor, Finance; Matias Carrasco Kind, Research Assistant Professor, Accountancy / Director, Data Science Research Services (DSRS)

---

## 1. The Opportunity

Every top business school is adding AI courses. Few are building shared infrastructure where teaching AI simultaneously produces reusable tools, generates research data, and builds institutional capability. Gies has the assets to do this — they are simply not yet connected.

The Magelli Office runs 200+ client projects per year. Six courses use Canvas MCP, an open-source AI toolkit with 90+ tools. DSRS operates a compute cluster with A100 GPUs, self-hosted LLM endpoints, the Atlas agentic framework, and iKompete — a competition platform for challenge submissions and peer evaluation — all described in Carrasco Kind (2023). Student organizations ([AgentLab](https://agentlab.illinihunt.org/), IBC, Disruption Lab) are actively building AI solutions. Yet these assets lack a shared challenge pipeline, compound skill library, or research instrumentation. This proposal funds the coordination layer that connects them — the AI Innovation Engine.

## 2. What We Propose

The AI Innovation Engine is a platform where faculty post real-world AI challenges and student teams build solutions using shared infrastructure. Every completed project deposits a reusable AI skill into a compound library that makes the next project faster and generates research data on human-AI collaboration. The Engine is an AI coordination layer on top of infrastructure that already exists.

**How it works.** Faculty post challenge briefs — operational automations, analytical tools, course infrastructure needs. DSRS's iKompete platform provides submission and evaluation infrastructure. Student builder teams ([AgentLab](https://agentlab.illinihunt.org/), Disruption Lab, course project teams) build solutions using Canvas MCP for LMS integration and DSRS for compute and data pipelines. Completed solutions enter the skill library; interaction data flows to the research dataset.

**The compound skill library.** Each completed project produces reusable components — prompt templates, data connectors, workflow patterns, evaluation rubrics. Next semester's students start with a richer toolkit. The library is itself a scholarly contribution: a novel model for compound experiential learning infrastructure, publishable in learning sciences and CSCW venues. This "Build to Learn, Learn to Build" model means students architect systems that serve the next cohort.

**Research data as byproduct.** The Engine instruments student-AI interactions at three levels: (a) task-level logs capturing tool usage and prompting strategies; (b) project-level metadata including challenge type, team composition, and outcomes; and (c) assessment data from rubric-based evaluations. All data collection follows IRB protocols with informed consent. Because students encounter the Engine as standard course infrastructure, the design avoids the selection bias inherent in laboratory AI studies (Dell'Acqua et al., 2023). Student-built skill library artifacts remain the intellectual property of their creators; interaction logs are collected only with consent and de-identified before analysis.

## 3. Research Questions

**RQ1:** How do business students develop AI collaboration skills over time, and what task characteristics predict effective versus ineffective human-AI partnerships?

**RQ2:** How does AI assistance affect the quality and confidence calibration of business decisions across functional domains?

RQ1 employs process mining of interaction sequences and regression analysis of skill trajectories across semesters. RQ2 uses experimental designs embedded in finance courses (Zhang), measuring decision quality and appropriate AI reliance. Beyond these, the Engine's instrumentation layer is designed so that additional Gies faculty can integrate their courses and collect data under their own IRB protocols — we build the infrastructure, they bring the research question.

## 6. Broader Impact and External Funding

**Shared research infrastructure.** The Engine lowers the barrier to AI education research from "build your own data collection apparatus" to "plug your course into the Engine." We build and maintain the instrumentation layer; faculty bring their research questions and IRB protocols. Faculty across Accountancy, Finance, Marketing, and Strategy can study AI-mediated decision-making, learning analytics, and technology adoption without duplicating infrastructure.

**Roadmap beyond the grant.** The Engine supports future extensions not funded here: corporate challenge sponsorships from Gies's DBC/CSP partners (30+ Fortune 500 companies), an Origin Ventures commercialization pathway for student-built tools, and a "Bring Your Own Challenge" model for Gies's 5,000+ online MBA students.

**Philanthropic potential.** The Engine positions Gies to attract alumni donors seeking to invest in AI infrastructure with measurable institutional impact — dedicated research infrastructure that bridges cutting-edge research with real-world business outcomes and demonstrates the execution capacity that discerning donors look for.

**External funding.** We intend to submit an NSF Cyberlearning and Future Learning Technologies proposal using Year 1 data and publications as preliminary results. Zhang's fintech industry connections provide additional pathways. The combination of philanthropic interest, corporate sponsor revenue, and federal grants creates a multi-source model to sustain the Engine beyond this initial investment.
